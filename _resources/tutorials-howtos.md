Our tutorials are short, task-shaped walkthroughs — the kind you reach for mid-deadline, not a semester course. They follow the arc of a real data story: **find it, get it, clean it, check it, show it.**

**Finding & getting data.** Where public data hides — agency portals, FOIA and Colorado CORA requests, the [Wayback Machine](https://web.archive.org/), and bulk downloads — and how to capture it before it moves. For public-records strategy we point to the [Reporters Committee's open-government guide](https://www.rcfp.org/open-government-guide/).

**Cleaning & validating.** Hands-on guides for [OpenRefine](https://openrefine.org/) (clustering messy categories), [pandas](https://pandas.pydata.org/docs/), and the [R tidyverse](https://www.tidyverse.org/) — plus how to validate *as you go* so errors surface early, not in print.

**Exploring & publishing.** Turning a clean table into a finding with [Datasette](https://datasette.io/), then into a chart a general audience can read (see [evaluated tooling](/resources/evaluated-tooling/) for the accessibility checks we apply).

We lean on the field's best open references rather than reinventing them — **[The Turing Way](https://the-turing-way.netlify.app/)** for reproducible, ethical data science, and the data-journalism community around **[NICAR](https://www.ire.org/training/conferences/)**.

Every walkthrough is written for the practitioner who has a deadline and a dataset, not a computer-science degree. Pair them with our [data documentation guides](/resources/data-documentation-guides/) and [tooling & code](/resources/tooling-code/), read the [Dispatch](/dispatch/) for worked examples, or [bring your dataset to the help desk](/get-involved/#desk).
