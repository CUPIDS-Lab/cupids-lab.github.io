New tools arrive faster than anyone can vet them. **Evaluated tooling** means we test AI models, visualizations, and geospatial systems against real public-interest use before we rely on — or recommend — them.

**AI & models.** We treat models as instruments that need calibration: we sample for accuracy, measure error rates, and document intended use and limits with **[Model Cards](https://arxiv.org/abs/1810.03993)** (Mitchell et al.), guided by the **[NIST AI Risk Management Framework](https://www.nist.gov/itl/ai-risk-management-framework)**. The caveats go up front, not in a footnote.

**Visualization.** A chart is an argument, so it has to be honest and readable. We design to web-accessibility standards (**[WCAG](https://www.w3.org/WAI/standards-guidelines/wcag/)**) and use perceptually sound, colorblind-safe palettes (**[ColorBrewer](https://colorbrewer2.org/)**), avoiding the distortions that mislead.

**Geospatial.** For mapping we lean on open, auditable GIS — **[QGIS](https://qgis.org/)** and **[PostGIS](https://postgis.net/)** — and watch for the classic traps: projection choices, normalizing by population, and the [modifiable areal unit problem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Modifiable_areal_unit_problem).

What we learn, we publish: the evaluation and the reasoning behind a "use this / avoid that" call, so a newsroom inherits the testing instead of repeating it.

This underwrites our [records-at-scale](/get-involved/help/records-at-scale/) and ["make a chart people can read"](/get-involved/help/visualize/) help-desk work. See our [tooling & code](/resources/tooling-code/) or [ask the help desk](/get-involved/#desk).
